## Version A: Reflective And Story-Driven

I started this project from a simple personal problem: sometimes I forgot where my assets were spread.

Some money was in accounts. Some was in e-wallets. Some was in investments. Some numbers looked big enough to catch my attention, while smaller assets became easier to ignore. The problem was not only about tracking money. It was about seeing the whole picture clearly.

That became the starting point for Worthly, a local-first iOS prototype for mapping assets, liabilities, transactions, and simple financial targets.

At the beginning, my biggest learning gap was not coding. I wanted to improve how I think about user flow, navigation, and especially layout.

Before this project, layout mostly meant “put things in a good place.” Through Worthly, I started to understand it differently. Layout is not just where a component sits. It is how information becomes easier to understand, how actions become clearer, and how people move through a product without feeling lost.

One moment that changed my direction was learning to look at Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines more carefully. I used to think HIG was mostly about visual design. Then I remembered the “I” stands for Interaction. That made me rethink many parts of the app: the tab structure, the add buttons, the editor screens, and even when a bottom sheet should become a full-screen flow.

The hardest design decision was around add and edit flows. At first, I used half-height sheets because they looked familiar. But for financial forms, the previous screen behind the sheet could become distracting. I changed the experience into focused full-screen editors so each task had clearer attention.

Accessibility also became part of the design process, not something added at the end. I worked on Dynamic Type, stronger contrast, readable amount text, accessible labels, and chart colors that stay understandable. For an app about money, readability is trust.

I also used a personal AI coding assistant as a thinking and implementation partner, not as an autopilot. I delegated parts like comparing my design with Apple HIG, refactoring SwiftUI files, generating dummy data, planning data structures, and writing code faster. But the product direction, user flow, navigation, and layout decisions still had to come from me.

This project became my small practice of 改善 (kaizen): continuous improvement. Not chasing perfection, but making the gap smaller one decision at a time.

Worthly is still a prototype. It is not perfect, and that is exactly why it became meaningful for me. The point was not only what I built, but how my thinking changed while building it.

The next step is to test it with people and see whether it really helps them understand where their money lives.

Tools used: Xcode, SwiftUI, SF Symbols, Sketch, GitHub, Simulator, and a personal AI coding assistant.
